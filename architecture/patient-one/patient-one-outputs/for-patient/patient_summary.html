‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

Why did my cancer become resistant to platinum chemotherapy?
Cancer cells can develop ways to survive treatment over time. Your tests show specific changes (like the drug pump ABCB1) that help cancer cells remove the chemotherapy. This is why we're switching to different medications that work in new ways.
What does having a BRCA1 mutation mean for my treatment?
Having a BRCA1 mutation actually opens up additional treatment options for you. PARP inhibitors are medications specifically designed to work well in patients with BRCA mutations. This mutation also means we should discuss genetic testing for your family members.
What is the expected outcome with these new treatments?
With the recommended combination therapy, we expect to see tumor shrinkage or stabilization in about 40-45% of patients. The goal is to control the cancer, maintain your quality of life, and extend the time before the cancer progresses. Average progression-free survival is 6-8 months, but many patients do better.
Will I have side effects?
All cancer treatments have potential side effects, but they vary from person to person. Common side effects of PARP inhibitors include fatigue, nausea, and low blood counts. We'll monitor you closely and have many ways to manage side effects to keep you comfortable.
Are there clinical trials I should consider?
Yes! There are several promising clinical trials for patients with BRCA1 mutations and platinum-resistant ovarian cancer. These include new combination therapies and novel immunotherapy approaches. We can discuss specific trials that you may qualify for.
